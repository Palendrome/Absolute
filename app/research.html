Seasonality of the Bottlenose Dolphin in Plettenberg Bay
Lead Institutions: Department of Environmental Affairs, Nelson Mandela University
This long-term monitoring project is done in collaboration with the Garden Route Dolphin Research Project
which aims to improve the understanding of seasonality and movement patterns of the
Indo-Pacific Bottlenose dolphin (Tursiops aduncus) in Plettenberg Bay.

The study was based on a catalogue of dorsal fin photo-identification images obtained from a platform of opportunity (boat-based whale watching vessel).

This form of identification allows individual dolphins to be identified by unique patterns in naturally occurring marks on their dorsal fins. Long-lasting marks (versus temporary markings) enables identification of individuals over long periods of time. These marks include fin deformities, unusual fin shapes and nicks in the trailing edge and peduncle. The acquisition of dorsal fin nicks and other long-lasting marks in small cetaceans is cumulative, therefore animals with these marks will tend to be the older individuals. Temporary markings include major and minor scratches, active and healed disease and white fin-fringe, which allow for identification of animals over short periods.
Photographs from a catalogue taken at different dates were matched to investigate temporal patterns of individuals. The study assessed patterns in residency and seasonality of reccurring individuals in the bay - whether the same individuals were frequently sighted in the bay or not, and whether any seasonal patterns of individuals occurred.

A photographic database, containing approximately 100 000 photographs dating back to 1991 was used as a means to record the movement patterns of the bottlenose dolphins. Usable photographs were cropped, sorted and individuals identified to form a database of dolphins frequenting Plettenberg Bay. Sally joined Alejandra and the Garden Route Dolphin Research Project to assist with the cataloguing of the photographs. This project formed the basis of the conceptualisation of the Visitors from the Deep children’s book. In order to keep monitoring the individuals visiting Plettenberg Bay, Sally will keep the record up to date, comparing newly acquired photographs with the existing historical catalogue (1991-2016).

Behavioural Effects of Swim-With-Seal Ecotourism within the Robberg MPA

Lead Institutions: Department of Environmental Affairs, Nelson Mandela University, CapeNature

Swim-with-seals ecotourism ventures are operating in two marine protected areas (MPAs) in South Africa; Robberg MPA in Plettenberg Bay and Table Mountain National Park MPA in Cape Town. Clients are afforded the opportunity to snorkel with Cape fur seals (Arctocephalus pusillus pusillus) in the animals’ natural environment and are able to observe their curious, and often hilarious, antics up close.If conducted responsibly, these ventures have the potential to benefit socioeconomic and conservation efforts; reckless conduct however can put the seals at risk of harassment, injury and death. In order to ensure the welfare of these animals, that they are not harassed or adversely affected in any way, the development of appropriate guidelines is required, also promoting the sustainable conduct of the operators.

The Department of Environmental Affairs (DEA) is the institute accountable for the establishment of protocols and the governing of such activities. Together with Nelson Mandela University (NMU) and CapeNature, they are working to understand the impact that these activities would have on the behaviour and welfare of the colony.
In an effort to understand how the seals react to these activities, Sally is currently carrying out observations on the Cape fur seal colony situated on the north side of the Robberg Peninsula. The aims of this study are to describe the swim-with-seal operations, investigate the short-term behavioural effects of the seal colony, and provide scientific recommendations towards “sustainable industry practice” and effective regulation. The situation and topography of the Robberg Nature Reserve, high cliffs overlooking the colony and adjacent waters, facilitates observations and counts of the animals onshore, in the shallows and further out to sea.

While on the mountain, Sally is also observing the presence and behaviour of a vagrant southern elephant seal bull (Mirounga leonine) as it interacts with the fur seals of the colony. This seal, known to locals as Solo, has been visiting the rocky shore of the Robberg Nature Reserve for several years; arriving from one of the sub-Antarctic islands to haul-out and moult.


Behaviour and Size of Great White Sharks utilising the Robberg MPA

The great white sharks (Carcharodon carcharias) that frequent the waters of Plettenberg Bay are of great interest to Sally and as such she is recording the presence, behaviour and size of the individuals swimming past the Cape fur seal (Arctocephalus pusillus pusillus) colony. The high cliffs provide a good vantage point from which to observe the sharks in the clear, shallow water. Great white sharks are apex predators and play a key role in maintaining healthy oceanic ecosystems. A protected species in South African waters since 1991, these sharks are recorded as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species and as such every effort should be made to ensure their survival. While seasonality is expected around the seal colony, an increase in white sharks in response to seal pup presence in the water, it will be interesting to record whether or not there is a seasonality of the size of shark utilising the waters of Plettenberg Bay. Not much is known about the lives of great white sharks; where they are born and where the juveniles grow up has remained a relative mystery until recently when nurseries were found off the coast of California and Mexico. It is important to identify possible nursery areas so as to afford them protection in an effort to promote the successful recruitment of juveniles and the overall survival of the species. The locations of where female white sharks give birth and how often they pup are key points to determine in ensuring the survivorship of this species. A number of juveniles (1-1.5 m) have been recorded in Plett and to know whether or not a nursery is present in the region will be vital in the planning and management of Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) along our coastline. Inshore mobbing of these white sharks, by the fur seals, is an interesting behaviour trait often recorded at the Robberg seal colony and one that Sally wishes to investigate further. Great white sharks are ambush predators with predations mostly taking place in deeper water, channels and along the fringes of kelp forests. When in the clear, shallow water close to the mountain however, the seals are able to spot the sharks and chase them off.
